# FUENTE-152: CMX Community Industry Standards — CMX Team

## Tesis Central

> **"Community is no longer experimental—it's a proven business function with established standards, career paths, and best practices. Companies that treat community professionally get professional results."**

---

## 1. Principios Fundamentales

> **P1: Community is a Distinct Discipline, Not Marketing**
> Community requires unique skills, metrics, and approaches. It's not social media management, not customer support, not marketing—it's its own discipline.
> *Fuente: [CMX Community Industry Report (CMX, 2023)]*
> *Contexto: When positioning community within organizations and building teams.*

> **P2: Community Professionals Need Career Pathways**
> Without clear progression, community talent leaves. Community managers need paths from junior to senior to director to VP.
> *Fuente: [Community Career Framework (CMX, 2023)]*
> *Contexto: When structuring community teams and retaining talent.*

> **P3: Measurement Must Be Business-Aligned**
- Vanity metrics (member count, engagement rate) don't prove business value. Community metrics must connect to acquisition, retention, revenue, or innovation.
> *Fuente: [Community Metrics Standards (CMX, 2023)]*
> *Contexto: When designing community measurement and reporting.*

> **P4: Executive Buy-In is Non-Negotiable for Success**
> Community without executive support fails. Community with executive support thrives. The difference is resources, patience, and integration.
> *Fuente: [Community Buy-In Framework (CMX, 2023)]*
> *Contexto: When pitching community programs and securing organizational support.*

> **P5: Community Operations Scale with Community Size**
- What works for 100 members breaks at 1,000. What works at 1,000 breaks at 10,000. Operations must evolve with community growth.
> *Fuente: [Community Operations Playbook (CMX, 2023)]*
> *Contexto: When planning community growth and operational infrastructure.*

## 2. Frameworks y Metodologías

### Framework 1: The Community Team Structure Framework

**Fuente:** [Community Career & Team Framework (CMX, 2023)]
**Propósito:** Structure community teams for scale and professionalism
**Cuándo usar:** When building community teams and defining roles

**Team Structure by Community Stage:**

**Stage 1: Solo Community Manager (0-1,000 members)**
- **Roles:**
  - Community Manager of One (all functions)
- **Responsibilities:**
  - Community strategy and execution
  - Member engagement and support
  - Content creation and curation
  - Event planning and hosting
  - Metrics tracking and reporting
- **Reports to:** Marketing, Product, or Founder (depends on business goal)
- **Budget:** Minimal (software, maybe small event budget)
- **Success Indicators:** Active members, engagement, early business metrics

**Stage 2: Core Community Team (1,000-10,000 members)**
- **Roles:**
  - Head of Community (strategy, team leadership)
  - Community Manager(s) (engagement, programming)
  - Community Coordinator (operations, logistics)
- **Responsibilities:**
  - **Head of Community:** Strategy, executive alignment, team management
  - **Community Managers:** Member relationships, content, events, programs
  - **Coordinator:** Admin, reporting, member support, operations
- **Reports to:** VP Marketing, VP Product, or Chief Community Officer (if exists)
- **Budget:** Software, events, programming, team salaries
- **Success Indicators:** Growth, engagement, business metrics (retention, revenue)

**Stage 3: Scalable Community Organization (10,000+ members)**
- **Roles:**
  - VP/Director of Community (organization strategy)
  - Community Managers (by segment or program)
  - Community Specialists (content, events, programs)
  - Community Operations (systems, processes, tools)
  - Community Analyst (data, insights, reporting)
  - (Optional) Community Advocates (super-user members)
- **Responsibilities:**
  - **VP/Director:** Organization strategy, executive partnership, team leadership
  - **Managers:** Specific community segments, major programs
  - **Specialists:** Execution excellence in specific domain
  - **Operations:** Infrastructure, scale, processes
  - **Analyst:** Measurement, insights, optimization
- **Reports to:** C-Suite (CMO, CPO, or CCO)
- **Budget:** Significant (team, events, platform, tools, programs)
- **Success Indicators:** Business impact metrics at scale

**Community Career Ladder:**

**Junior Community Manager**
- 0-2 years experience
- Execution of programs and engagement
- Learning community fundamentals

**Community Manager**
- 2-5 years experience
- Owns programs or member segments
- Independent execution and strategy input

**Senior Community Manager**
- 5-8 years experience
- Leads major initiatives, mentors juniors
- Strategic contribution to community direction

**Head/Director of Community**
- 8+ years experience
- Team leadership, cross-functional strategy
- Business outcomes ownership

**VP of Community**
- 10+ years experience
- Organization-wide community strategy
- Executive leadership, industry thought leadership

**Output esperado:** Team structure that scales with community and organizational maturity

### Framework 2: The Community Metrics Hierarchy

**Fuente:** [Community Metrics Standards (CMX, 2023)]
**Propósito:** Measure community with business-aligned metrics
**Cuándo usar:** When designing community measurement and reporting

**The 4 Levels of Community Metrics:**

**Level 1: Health Metrics (Is Community Healthy?)**
- **Member Growth:** Total members, new members, churn rate
- **Engagement Rate:** Active members / total members, posts, comments, reactions
- **Member Sentiment:** NPS, satisfaction, qualitative feedback
- **Content Health:** Content created, response rates, quality scores
- **Use Case:** Internal community health monitoring, monthly reporting
- **Benchmark:** 30%+ monthly active rate is healthy

**Level 2: Program Metrics (Are Activities Working?)**
- **Event Performance:** Attendance, satisfaction, repeat attendance rate
- **Content Performance:** Views, shares, engagement per piece
- **Program Completion:** % who complete onboarding, courses, challenges
- **Member Actions:** Referrals sent, UGC created, mentorship sessions
- **Use Case:** Program optimization, resource allocation
- **Benchmark:** Track week-over-week improvements

**Level 3: Business Metrics (Is Community Driving Business Value?)**
- **Acquisition:** Members invited, referral rate, community-attributed leads
- **Retention:** Community member vs non-member retention, churn reduction
- **Revenue:** Community-generated revenue, LTV of community members
- **Innovation:** Ideas from community implemented, feedback impact
- **Use Case:** Executive reporting, business case, ROI calculation
- **Benchmark:** Community members should have 2-3x higher LTV

**Level 4: Strategic Metrics (Is Community Achieving Strategic Goals?)**
- **Market Position:** Community vs competitor communities, thought leadership
- **Brand Advocacy:** NPS of community members, UGC volume, reviews
- **Product Direction:** % of roadmap influenced by community
- **Partnership Value:** Co-created initiatives, ecosystem development
- **Use Case:** Strategic planning, board reporting, long-term direction
- **Benchmark:** Community as competitive differentiator

**Measurement Best Practices:**
- Start with Level 1 (health), add Level 2 (programs), then Level 3 (business)
- Don't report all metrics to all audiences—executives get Level 3-4, team gets Level 1-2
- Set baselines before initiatives, measure delta, not absolute numbers
- Contextualize metrics with qualitative insights (stories, member quotes)

**Output esperado:** Measurement framework that proves community business value

### Framework 3: The Community Platform Selection Matrix

**Fuente:** [Community Platform Guide (CMX, 2023)]
**Propósito:** Select right community platform for your needs
**Cuándo usar:** When choosing community technology

**Platform Categories:**

**Category A: Real-Time Chat Platforms**
- **Examples:** Slack, Discord
- **Best for:** Fast-paced conversation, real-time collaboration, developer communities
- **Pros:** Familiar interface, real-time, low friction
- **Cons:** Message fatigue, poor search, hard to catch up, noisy
- **Cost:** Low to medium ($$
- **Size:** Up to ~5,000 active members before scale issues

**Category B: Forums/Message Boards**
- **Examples:** Discourse, Circle, Mighty Networks
- **Best for:** Deep discussion, knowledge base, async conversation
- **Pros:** Better for search, threads, slow-burn discussions, SEO
- **Cons:** Higher friction, slower pace, less immediate
- **Cost:** Medium ($$)
- **Size:** Scales to 50,000+ members

**Category C: Social Groups**
- **Examples:** Facebook Groups, LinkedIn Groups, Reddit
- **Best for:** Casual communities, low budget, testing concepts
- **Pros:** Free, built-in audience, familiar to users
- **Cons:** Limited control, no data ownership, platform dependency
- **Cost:** Free
- **Size:** Unlimited (platform handles scale)

**Category D: Community Platforms (All-in-One)**
- **Examples:** Circle, Mighty Networks, Bettermode
- **Best for:** Membership communities, courses + community, paid communities
- **Pros:** Ownership, features, customization, monetization
- **Cons:** Higher cost, can feel generic, learning curve
- **Cost:** Medium to High ($$-$$$)
- **Size:** Up to ~25,000 members

**Decision Framework:**

**Question 1: What's your primary community goal?**
- Fast collaboration → Chat (Slack/Discord)
- Knowledge/discussion → Forum (Discourse/Circle)
- Audience building → Social Groups
- Membership/paid → Platform (Circle/Mighty)

**Question 2: What's your budget?**
- No budget → Social Groups
- Low budget → Discord (free), Slack (paid)
- Medium budget → Circle, Discourse
- High budget → Custom or enterprise solutions

**Question 3: What's your technical comfort level?**
- Want it to just work → Slack, Facebook Groups
- Willing to tinker → Discord, Discourse
- Want full control → Circle, Mighty Networks

**Question 4: Do you need data ownership?**
- Yes (community is strategic) → Owned platform
- No (community is experimental) → Rented platform

**Output esperado:** Platform decision with clear rationale

## 4. Criterios de Decisión

### Trade-off: Generalist vs Specialist Community Managers

**Fuente:** [Team Structure Guide (CMX, 2023)]

**Opción A: Generalist Community Managers**
- **Descripción:** Community managers who do everything (engagement, content, events, ops)
- **Ventajas:** Flexibility, holistic view, simpler team
- **Desventajas:** Burnout risk, harder to scale depth, jack-of-all-trades
- **Mejor para:** Early stage communities (0-5,000 members)

**Opción B: Specialist Community Managers**
- **Descripción:** Community managers focused on specific domains (content, events, member segments)
- **Ventajas:** Deep expertise, scale better, less burnout, higher quality
- **Desventajas:** More complex team, coordination overhead, higher cost
- **Mejor para:** Mature communities (10,000+ members)

**Recomendación del experto:** Start with generalists, add specialists as community scales. At 10,000+ members, you should have specialists for major functions (content, events, programming) plus generalists for member engagement.

## 5. Anti-patrones

### Anti-patrón 1: Hiring Social Media Managers as Community Managers

**Fuente:** [Hiring Mistakes (CMX, 2023)]
**Qué es:** Assuming social media skills translate to community management
**Por qué la gente lo hace:** Seems similar, both involve social platforms
**Consecuencias:** Broadcast mindset, lack of member relationship skills, high churn
**Cómo evitarlo:** Hire for relationship-building, psychology, facilitation skills—not content creation
**Qué hacer en su lugar:** Look for customer success, psychology, facilitation backgrounds. Teach social media, can't teach relationship skills.
